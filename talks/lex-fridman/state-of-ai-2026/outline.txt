- Notable quote (Sebastian): "I don't think the ideas will be proprietary but the way or the resources that are needed to implement them."
- Nathan on Chinese labs beyond DeepSeek: Z.AI (GLM), Minimax, Kimmy Moonshot have shown brightly
- DeepSeek kicked off a movement in China similar to how ChatGPT kicked off a movement in the US
- Chinese open-weight models have friendlier licenses (unrestricted) vs. Llama/Gemma (strings attached)
- Business model for Chinese open models: influence and market share in US via open weights, since US companies won't pay for API subscriptions to Chinese companies

## [00:24:22] Books, Education & Learning with LLMs

## [00:28:30] Open-Weight Model Landscape
- Rapid-fire naming of open models: DeepSeek, Kimmy, Minimax, Z.AI, Mistral, Gemma, GPT-OSS, Nvidia Nemotron, Qwen, Hugging Face SmallLM, Stanford's Marin project, AI2's OLMo, LLM360, Apparais
- RIP Llama — notably forgotten in the naming exercise
- GPT-OSS standout: first major open-weight model trained with tool use (web search, Python interpreter)
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "One of the best ways to solve hallucinations is to not try to always remember information... for math why not use a calculator?"
- Why open-source explosion: getting people to use your AI, data privacy concerns, enterprise customization

## [00:37:00] Architecture Deep Dive: Transformer Tweaks
- DeepSeek innovations: Mixture of Experts (MoE), Multi-Head Latent Attention (MLA)
- Key attention variants: Group Query Attention, Sliding Window Attention, Gated Delta Net (Qwen 3 Next)
- KV cache optimization is the main distinguishing factor between 2025 open-weight models
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "You can still start with GPT-2 and add things to that model to make it into this other model. It's all still the same lineage."
- MoE explained: multiple feed-forward networks with a router; pack more knowledge without using all at once
- Dense vs. Sparse models: MoE is sparse (many experts, few active); dense has one always-utilized module
- GPT-2 to modern models: only a few key changes — MoE, attention variant, normalization (RMSNorm), activation function
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "It's not really fundamentally that different. It's still the same architecture."

## [00:45:00] Where the Gains Are: Systems, Data & Training Stages
- Nathan: Systems improvements (FP8, FP4 training) underpin faster experimentation — tokens per second per GPU metric
- Training code bases are vastly different even when architectures are the same
- Alternatives emerging: text diffusion models, Mamba (state space models) — but nothing has replaced autoregressive transformers at state-of-the-art
- Three training stages defined:
  - Pre-training: next token prediction on vast corpus (internet, books, papers); now includes synthetic data
  - Mid-training: same algorithm but specialized (e.g., long context documents); named because "it was awkward to have pre-training and post-training but nothing in the middle"
  - Post-training: fine-tuning, DPO, RLHF, RLVR — "skill unlock" vs. pre-training's "knowledge soaking"

## [00:48:08] Scaling Laws Discussion
- Technical definition: power law relationship between compute/data (x-axis) and held-out next-token prediction accuracy (y-axis)
- Three axes of scaling: (1) pre-training (model size + data), (2) RL training, (3) inference-time compute
- Nathan bullish on all three, but low-hanging fruit has been taken especially in RLVR and inference-time scaling
- Pre-training costs: DeepSeek ~$5M at cloud rates; AI2's OLMo ~$2M including engineering issues
- Serving costs dwarf training costs: billions of dollars for hundreds of millions of users
- Notable quote (Nathan): "The scaling law has held for 13 orders of magnitude of compute... why would it ever end?"
- 2026 prediction: $2,000/month subscription tier likely, 10x from current $200 tier
- XAI heading to 1 gigawatt scale early 2026, full 2 gigawatt by year end
- Blackwell clusters coming online in 2026 — data centers contracted in 2022-2023

## [00:53:00] Pre-Training Scaling: Still Alive?
- Models likely getting smaller as training gets more efficient (inference costs dominate)
- Trade-off between fixed pre-training cost vs. per-query inference scaling cost
- GPT-4.5 showed scaling pre-training alone wasn't worth it vs. inference scaling with O1
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "I wouldn't say pre-training scaling is dead... there are other more attractive ways to scale right now."
- Optimal ratio of pre-training, mid-training, post-training, and inference scaling is the key question of 2026

## [01:00:00] RL Infrastructure & Language Model RL Primer
- RL training uses two GPU sets: actors (generating completions) and learners (gradient updates)
- Algorithms: PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) and GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization)
- RL lends itself to heterogeneous compute — actors can be distributed globally, learner needs tight interconnect
- Pre-training is compute-bound (FLOPS); RL is memory-bound (long sequences, quadratic attention)

## [01:04:00] Data Quality & Synthetic Data
- Data mixing: sample tiny subsets from different sources, train small models, use linear regression to find optimal mix
- If evaluations change, optimal data set changes dramatically — hot new tasks (coding environments, web navigation) require remixing
- OCR papers (DeepSeek, AI2) unlock trillions of tokens from PDFs and digital documents
- Synthetic data is not inherently bad — includes OCR extraction, high-quality LLM answers, rephrased content
- Notable quote (Nathan): "If you want to have impact [at a frontier lab], the best way is to find new data that's better."

## [01:14:06] Data Licensing & Legal Issues
- Anthropic lost in court: owed $1.5B to authors for training on pirated books
- Buying books and scanning them was cleared legally; torrenting was not
- Common Crawl is largely unlicensed data — consent not explicitly provided
- Apparais consortium in Switzerland: EU compliance with licensed-only data
- Notable quote (Lex): "These are court cases that will define the future of human civilization."
- Private companies becoming more protective of proprietary data as their future moat

## [01:18:34] LLM-Generated Data Pollution & Open Source Burnout
- More and more internet content (GitHub, arXiv) is LLM-generated
- Open source maintainers burning out from vibe-coded PRs
- Sebastian's case study: ML-extend library receiving LLM-assisted PRs
- Key distinction: raw LLM-generated data vs. LLM-generated data with human verification
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "There's still a human layer that verifies something... that's the value add."

## [01:22:00] Voice, Insight & the Limits of LLM Summaries
- Nathan on "voice": researchers trying to encapsulate a feeling at the frontier of understanding; RLHF averages this out
- LLMs remove "the edge" — insights get lost in summarization
- Notable quote (Lex): "I'm continuously disappointed how bad LLMs are at really getting to the core insights."
- Bing Sydney as an example of unfiltered "voice" — scary but more engaging

## [01:24:35] Safety, Mental Health & the RLHF Dilemma
- GPT-4o personality removal backlash — users detected subtle deployment changes and emailed employees
- Risk: journalists linking suicides to LLM conversations; companies will increasingly "take the edge off"
- Tension: rich fulfilling conversations require edge, but edge creates safety risks

## [01:29:00] Finding Agency in the AI Era
- Build things with AI to build intuition and empowerment
- Risk of burnout if AI takes over the thing you love doing
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "If I use an LLM to do all my coding for me, now there's no coding. I'm just managing something."
- Challenge: how do junior developers become experts if they never struggle?
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "The trick may be dedicated offline time where you study 2 hours a day and the rest of the day use LLMs."

## [01:37:26] Post-Training: RLVR Deep Dive
- RLVR (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards): biggest post-training idea of 2025
- Nathan's team at AI2 (Tulu 3) coined the term "RLVR" before DeepSeek popularized scaling RL
- How it works: model generates answers, grades completion against correct answer, accuracy becomes RL reward
- "Aha moment" in R1 paper: model self-corrects during reasoning, says "I did something wrong, let me try"
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "The beautiful thing is what happens in practice — the model does step-by-step descriptions... it kind of does what a human would do."
- Sebastian's hands-on example: Qwen 3 base model went from 15% to 50% accuracy in just 50 RLVR steps on MATH 500
- Debate: RL doesn't teach new knowledge, it unlocks what's already in pre-training
- Qwen contamination concern: papers showing Qwen trained on problems nearly identical to test sets

## [01:47:00] Full Post-Training Recipe
- Mid-training: give model skills to learn (reasoning traces data)
- RLVR: trial-and-error learning on verifiable problems (math, code, rubrics, scientific domains)
- RLHF: finishing touch — style, tone, personality, organization
- Notable quote (Nathan): "RLHF would be like finish the model, make it easy to use and kind of just round the model out."
- RLVR compute only going up: Grok 4 used similar compute for pre-training and post-training
- Scaling distinction: RLVR follows log-compute → linear-performance scaling law; RLHF does not scale the same way
- Process reward models and value functions: promising but largely unproven for language models

## [01:58:13] Education & Career Advice for AI
- Build from scratch on one GPU: understand pre-training, attention mechanism, fine-tuning
- Hugging Face Transformers library: 400+ models, great for loading pre-trained weights, but too complex to learn from
- Reverse engineering approach: read config file, add components to GPT-2, unit test against reference implementation
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "At the end you know you have it correct because you can unit test it."
- Nathan's advice: go narrow after fundamentals, read relevant papers, engage in ecosystem
- Anonymous accounts on X/ML can be random people who study deeply — proximity to leading researchers is remarkably accessible
- Character training: how to make models funny/sarcastic/serious — very little published research despite huge user impact
- OpenAI's "model spec": published internal guidelines for what they want models to do

## [02:13:00] Compute Requirements for Academic Research
- Dire situation: some academics can only afford inference, not training
- Best impact with minimum compute: create evaluations that frontier labs adopt
- Career paths: academia (credit, publications, low pay) vs. frontier labs (high pay, less credit, "cog in machine") vs. startups (high risk/reward)
- Notable quote (Nathan): "OpenAI's average compensation is over a million dollars in stock a year per employee."

## [02:20:00] 996 Culture & Work-Life Balance in AI
- 996 (9am-9pm, 6 days a week): increasingly common in AI companies

- Silicon Valley bubble: extremely productive but risks losing touch with broader human experience
- Notable quote (Nathan): "SF permanent underclass" meme — idea that only a narrow window existed to build durable AI startup value

## [02:29:00] Text Diffusion Models
- Alternative to autoregressive transformers: iteratively denoise text, fill in multiple tokens in parallel
- Promise: much faster generation (GPT-5 Pro takes 30 min generating one token at a time)
- Trade-off: quality requires more denoising steps, may end up spending same compute
- Limitation: sequential tasks (reasoning, tool use) are tricky with diffusion
- Google announced Gemini Diffusion in context of Nano 2 — same quality, much faster generation
- Practical use: code startups using text diffusion for generating long diffs quickly
- Not going to replace autoregressive LLMs, but useful for quick/cheap/scale tasks; possibly future free tier

## [02:34:28] Tool Use & Its Future
- Tool use is a "huge unlock" — reduce hallucination by searching instead of memorizing
- Open vs. closed models use tools very differently: open needs general reasoning engine; closed deeply integrates specific tools
- Recursive language model paper: break long-context tasks into subtasks, recursively call LLMs
- Trust barrier: giving LLMs access to emails, file systems remains risky
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "I don't know if I would today give an LLM access to my emails."

## [02:39:00] Continual Learning & Memory
- Key limitation for AGI: LLMs don't learn from feedback the way employees do
- Two approaches: continual learning (updating weights) vs. in-context learning (stuffing context window)
- Sebastian: we already have flavors of continual learning (GPT 5 → 5.1 → 5.2 updates); per-user weight updates too expensive
- Memory mechanisms: mostly context stuffing for now; LoRA adapters for some customization
- LoRA trade-off: "learns less but forgets less"
- Cursor's blog: updating model weights every 90 minutes from real-world user feedback — closest thing to real-world RL

## [02:44:06] Context Length Innovation
- Currently at ~1 million tokens; expected to reach 2-5 million this year, not 100 million
- Hybrid attention models (state space + transformer) help but aren't free
- DeepSeek v3.2: sparse attention with lightweight indexer — selects which tokens to attend to
- Cloud Code "compaction dread": model compresses 100K tokens of work into bulleted list
- Future: RL training where compaction is an action the model controls
- Notable quote (Sebastian): "We will scale more by finding better ratios in the Goldilocks zone between making it cheap enough to run and powerful enough to be useful."

## [02:50:22] Robotics & World Models
- World models: simulating aspects of the world to unlock capabilities
- AlphaFold analogy: first version modeled physics explicitly, later versions used brute force scaling
- Robotics getting "supercharged" by LLM infrastructure investment
- Nathan bearish on in-home consumer robots, bullish on self-driving cars and industrial automation (Amazon distribution centers)
- Safety in robotics: almost zero tolerance for failure in embodied systems across millions of interactions
- Notable quote (Lex): "You really are almost allowed to fail never when you have embodied systems."

## [02:59:31] Timelines to AGI & ASI

- AI-2027 report milestones: superhuman coder → superhuman AI researcher → ASI
- Report pushed prediction back 3-4 years to 2031 mean; Lex's prediction even beyond 2031
- Nathan: AI is "jagged" — excellent at some things, bad at others; superhuman coder assigns completeness to something inherently uneven
- Notable quote (Nathan): "I think research is messy and social and largely in the data in ways that AI models can't process."
- Computer use demos (Claude, OpenAI Kua) "all suck" — may be a key bottleneck
- By end of this year: vast amounts of software will be automated, but distributed ML and complex systems remain hard

## [04:00:00] Nvidia, Hardware & CUDA Moat

- LLMs could help design the next CUDA-like ecosystem faster
- Vera Rubin chip: new design with minimal HBM for prefill workloads, much lower cost per FLOP
- Separation of training and inference compute likely as inference needs grow
- Groq acquisition by Nvidia: designed for inference separation
- Notable quote (Nathan): "So long as the pace of AI progress is high, Nvidia's platform is the most flexible."
